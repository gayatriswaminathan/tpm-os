# Launch Announcement (+ video brief)

Turn a program moment into communication people actually watch and remember — a tight written announcement, plus a short video script ready for an AI video tool or an editor.

## When to use this skill

Most TPM artifacts go to a few people who have to read them. This skill is for the rare, high-broadcast moments where engagement matters more than detail:

- A program or feature **launch** (internal or external)
- A **kickoff** — aligning a whole team on the why and the goal
- A **process change** everyone needs to adopt (new intake, new cadence)
- An **enablement / onboarding** topic that's easier shown than written

If only a handful of stakeholders need it, use `status-update` instead. Use this when you need a room — or an org — to *get it* fast.

## Inputs to ask for

1. **The moment** — what's launching / changing / being taught.
2. **Audience** — internal team, whole org, leadership, or external.
3. **The one thing** they must take away.
4. **Key facts** — what's new, why it matters, what to do next.
5. **Tone** — celebratory (launch), motivating (kickoff), practical (enablement).
6. **Format target** — written only, or written + a short video (15–30s)?

## Operating principles

- **One message per piece.** A broadcast that says three things says nothing. Pick the single takeaway and build everything around it.
- **Lead with "what's in it for them."** Not "we shipped X" but "you can now do Y." Audiences tune in for the benefit, not the milestone.
- **Make the next action obvious.** Every announcement ends with one clear, specific thing to do (try it, read it, join it).
- **Short is the whole point.** A launch video is 15–30 seconds; an announcement is a few lines. If it needs scrolling, it won't get watched.
- **Honest, not hype.** Real benefit, real limits. Overselling a launch erodes the trust you'll need for the next one.
- **Show, don't tell, in video.** The script directs *visuals and on-screen text*, not a talking head reading the press release.

## Output structure

### Part A — Written announcement

```
[Headline — the benefit, in one line]
[2–3 sentences: what's new, why it matters to the audience, what changed]
👉 [One clear next action]
```

### Part B — Video script & brief (if requested)

A 15–30 second short, scene by scene. For each scene give: the on-screen text, the visual direction, and timing. Keep it to 3–5 scenes.

```
# [Title] — 0:20 launch video
Audience: [...] · Tone: [...] · Aspect: [16:9 / 9:16 / 1:1]

Scene 1 (0:00–0:04) — The hook
  On-screen text: "[the pain or the promise]"
  Visual: [what we see — keep it concrete]

Scene 2 (0:04–0:12) — The reveal
  On-screen text: "[what's new / the benefit]"
  Visual: [...]

Scene 3 (0:12–0:18) — Proof / how
  On-screen text: "[a number, a before/after, a quick demo beat]"
  Visual: [...]

Scene 4 (0:18–0:20) — The ask
  On-screen text: "[the one next action]"
  Visual: [logo / CTA card]

Voiceover (optional): [one or two short lines]
Music/feel: [e.g. upbeat, confident, calm]
```

The video script is **tool-agnostic** — it's a brief you can hand to an AI video generator (Higgsfield, Sora, etc.) or a human editor. Each scene's "Visual" line is the prompt.

## Tone

Warm, confident, concrete. This is the one place a TPM gets to make people *feel* something about the work — use it, but stay honest.

## Quality bar before returning

- Is there exactly **one** takeaway?
- Does it lead with the audience's benefit, not the team's milestone?
- Is there a single, obvious next action?
- Is the video 3–5 scenes, ≤30 seconds, with concrete visual direction per scene?
- Could someone hand Part B straight to a video tool and get something usable?
